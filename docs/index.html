LLMs as Tools for Objective Evolution

A research direction about using language models where they are strong, while keeping optimization where math is strong.

There is a growing line of work that treats LLMs not only as models that answer questions, but as components inside optimization loops. This is already happening in different forms. In OPRO, the LLM sees previous candidate solutions and their scores, then proposes new candidates. In prompt optimization, the LLM rewrites prompts based on performance feedback. In TextGrad-style methods, a language model produces textual feedback that plays the role of a gradient for text variables. In evolutionary methods such as language model crossover and Evolution through Large Models, the LLM acts like a mutation or crossover operator. In Eureka, the LLM generates and revises reward code for reinforcement learning.

I think this is one of the most interesting directions for LLMs. But I do not think the best framing is that LLMs should replace optimizers. Optimization is one of the places where math, gradients, solvers, sampling methods, and numerical algorithms have built a lot of rigor. If we have a well-defined objective and a reliable optimizer, we should not throw that away just because we have a language model.

The more interesting direction to me is to use LLMs around optimization. They can propose semantically meaningful moves, write candidate rules, summarize failure traces, generate tests, translate human feedback into constraints, and help us revise the objective. Then another system can check, score, simulate, solve, or optimize those proposals.

This matters because in many real problems the hard part is not only finding the optimum. The hard part is that the real objective is not fully written down. The target may be subjective, changing, tacit, or only partially formalized. We may not know how to write the global loss, but we can often say local things that are true: "these two examples should behave similarly", "this answer violates the style I want", "this agent should not talk to that agent unless it has evidence", "this cluster is too mixed", "this prompt is over-optimizing for correctness and losing clarity".

These local statements are not the full objective. But they are useful constraints. They can be turned into soft penalties, tests, validators, routing rules, prompts, or typed edits to an optimization problem. My view is that LLMs are a natural tool for this translation layer.

The core idea is: use LLMs to translate local, natural-language guidance into typed and validated objective edits, then let rigorous optimization methods solve the resulting problem.

The problem with fixed objectives

In standard optimization we usually assume there is a fixed objective L(x). We then design an algorithm to minimize it. In gradient-based optimization we use local derivatives. In derivative-free optimization we may estimate directions, use populations, use surrogate models, or search over candidates.

It would be inaccurate to say that optimization always uses the same loss in a naive way. There are many serious methods that change or shape the objective over time: continuation methods, curriculum learning, annealing, adaptive penalties, augmented Lagrangians, and trust-region methods. So the issue is not simply that the loss is fixed.

The issue I care about is more specific. Most of these objective changes are hand-designed and content-blind. They do not read the problem, understand what the user meant, look at a failure trace, and propose a new local rule. They give us mathematical control, but not much semantic bandwidth.

On the other side, using an LLM as the optimizer itself gives semantic bandwidth but loses numerical rigor. This is also why I do not think the right goal is "LLMs replace optimizers". LLMs are good at interpreting text, writing structured code, proposing hypotheses, and using background knowledge. They are not the best tool for high-dimensional numerical search, stable convergence, or exact constraint satisfaction.

A general formulation

A more useful formulation is to split the system into two levels.

At iteration t: We have variables x_t and a current objective L_t(x). We run a rigorous optimizer to improve x_t under L_t. We observe feedback, traces, failures, or human comments s_t. An LLM maps s_t into a typed objective edit Delta C_t. A validator checks whether Delta C_t is legal, consistent, and safe. We update the objective: L_{t+1}(x) = L_base(x) + sum_{c in C_t union Delta C_t} lambda_c phi_c(x) Then the rigorous optimizer continues.

In this view, the LLM does not need to know the optimum. It does not need to compute a gradient. It does not need to directly manipulate every parameter. Its job is to propose local rules that are meaningful, typed, and checkable.

This is close in spirit to constraint satisfaction, but I would be careful not to claim that every optimization problem is literally becoming an SMT problem. SMT and optimization modulo theories are formal solver settings with their own guarantees. The broader idea here is a softer and more general one: optimization becomes an evolving soft-constraint problem, where local constraints are authored from language and then compiled into a mathematical or executable form.

Why this is different from the alternatives

Approach What it gives us Where it is limited
Classical fixed-objective optimization Rigor, convergence tools, scale, reproducibility Requires the objective to already be formalized
Continuation, curriculum, annealing, adaptive penalties Principled objective shaping over time The shaping is usually hand-designed and not semantically aware
Pure LLM optimization Flexible language interface and broad prior knowledge No reliable numerical geometry, weak convergence story, high variance
LLM-written free-form objective code High expressivity Large failure surface: invalid code, reward hacking, unsafe objective edits
Typed LLM objective edits plus rigorous optimization Natural-language freedom at the boundary, mathematical rigor in the core The typed schema limits what the LLM can express

This last row is the direction I find promising. The LLM is used for what it is good at: text interpretation, hypothesis generation, mapping loose intent into structured forms, and sometimes proposing code or tests. The optimizer is used for what it is good at: search, gradients, constraints, numerical stability, and scale.

The important design principle is the typed interface. The LLM should not be allowed to freely corrupt the optimization loop. It should emit a small object that can be checked. A bad LLM output should become one bad soft rule that can be weighted, rejected, overridden, or rolled back. It should not become an unbounded update to the entire system.

A simple example: clustering

The clustering project in this repository is a small example of this idea. Clustering is a good example because the "correct" clustering is often subjective. The same set of documents can be clustered by topic, tone, user intent, risk, product area, or any other latent concept. There may not be a single global objective that captures what the user means.

In the code, the system starts with embeddings and k-means. That is the rigorous part. Then the user gives natural-language feedback: "items 0 and 4 should be together", "item 6 should not be with item 2", "rename this cluster", "emphasize genre rather than actor names", "this cluster is too mixed".

The LLM does not recluster the dataset by itself. It maps the message into a typed FeedbackAction. The action can be a must-link, cannot-link, rename, feature emphasis, miscluster flag, subcluster request, or outlier assignment. The constraint store checks consistency. The differentiable loss then changes, and the adapter is trained with Adam before k-means runs again.

Natural language feedback -> LLM parser -> typed FeedbackAction -> checked ConstraintStore -> updated FeedbackLoss -> Adam and k-means -> new clustering -> more feedback

This is not the most general possible version of the idea. It is deliberately constrained. That is the point. The LLM gets the freedom of language, but the optimizer gets a typed, bounded, mathematical object. The result is a hybrid system: semantic flexibility at the boundary and numerical rigor in the core.

A larger direction: optimizing agentic systems as graphs

A more general and exciting application is agentic systems. Suppose an agentic system is a graph. Nodes are LLM calls, tools, retrievers, classifiers, memory modules, critics, and models. Edges are communication channels and control flow. The performance of the system depends on many different kinds of parameters.

Some of these parameters are rigorous. Model weights, LoRA adapters, retriever embeddings, routing probabilities, and edge-selection policies can often be optimized with gradients, reinforcement learning, Bayesian optimization, or other numerical methods.

Other parts are loose. Prompts, tool descriptions, agent messages, role definitions, critique instructions, and communication protocols are textual objects. They do not have a clean vector space where ordinary gradients apply. Yet they matter enormously.

This gives a unified formulation:

Agentic system = graph G = (V, E) Hard parameters: model weights, embeddings, routers, edge probabilities, retrieval indices Soft parameters: prompts, messages, tool descriptions, communication rules, critique policies Goal: optimize system performance while assigning each block to the optimizer that fits it best.

The same philosophy applies. We should not force one optimization method to solve all parts of the graph. For hard parameters, use rigorous optimizers. For soft parameters, use LLMs to propose edits, rules, tests, and local communication constraints. Between the two, use typed interfaces and validators.

This can become a general view of agent optimization: not prompt optimization alone, not model optimization alone, not graph search alone, but heterogeneous optimization over a system whose parts have different mathematical character.

How this relates to current papers

There are several papers that support this direction. OPRO shows that LLMs can act as black-box optimizers over natural-language solutions, although the authors are clear that this is not meant to beat specialized numerical solvers. TextGrad and earlier textual-gradient work show how natural-language feedback can be used as a kind of gradient for text variables. Eureka shows LLMs evolving reward code for reinforcement learning. HypoGeniC shows an explicit loop of hypothesis generation, validation, and refinement.

For agentic systems, GPTSwarm is especially relevant because it models language agents as optimizable graphs, with node-level prompt optimization and edge-level graph optimization. DSPy is another important example because it treats language-model programs as optimizable pipelines. Automated Design of Agentic Systems and AFlow push the idea toward automatic workflow and agent design.

There are also papers that warn us not to overclaim. Work revisiting OPRO on smaller models shows that LLMs can be weak optimizers when model capacity is limited. OPRO itself reports context-window and landscape limitations. Work on multi-objective prompt optimization shows that textual gradients do not behave like numerical gradients: they do not have magnitudes, inner products, or projection operations in the same way. This is exactly why I think the hybrid view is important. The LLM should not be trusted as the whole optimizer. It should be placed where its failure modes are contained.

The research question

The question is not whether LLMs are better optimizers than Adam, CMA-ES, SMT solvers, or Bayesian optimization. In many cases they are not. The better question is:

Can LLMs help us evolve the objective, constraints, tests, and communication rules of a system while rigorous optimizers handle the parts that have mathematical structure?

If the answer is yes, then LLMs become tools for objective evolution. They help move between the language of human intent and the language of optimization. The optimizer remains the optimizer. But the objective becomes something that can be discussed, revised, localized, and improved over time.

I think this is a promising direction because many useful systems are not just models anymore. They are graphs of models, prompts, tools, validators, agents, and communication channels. Some parts are mathematical. Some parts are textual. Some parts are subjective. The right optimizer for such systems should not be a single algorithm. It should be a composition of optimizers, with LLMs acting as the semantic layer that proposes local rules and translates human intent into typed objective edits.

Running example: this repository's clustering system implements a minimal version of the idea. The broader direction is heterogeneous optimization for agentic systems.
